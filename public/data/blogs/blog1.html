Looking back and why I chose to major in computer science

April 29, 2026

There were many factors that led me to choosing to major in computer science in college, but I swear money wasn’t one of them.

Intro: Hey everyone! I’m Aaron, an incoming junior studying computer science and AI at University of Michigan Dearborn. I’m currently a technical lead for Google Developer Groups on Campus and a dev lead for Hack Dearborn 5. I’m still building and finding my niche but I want to do something along the lines of fullstack dev, AI engineering, and possibily low-level/embedded in the future.

Earliest exposure to computer science

I remember when my uncle came to visit my family in Michigan and one time he was fixing my parents 10+ year old Macbook, which still works to this day. I was aware that he was tech savvy and that he carried a lot of technology with him (the newest android, techy cameras, etc.)

I also remember a moment where he visited us in Michigan again, and he brought his high tech drone with a camera and flew it past my neighborhood. I was definitely fascinated by it.

Being with my uncle inspired me to embrace technology more. When I started 7th grade (my transition to middle school for our district), I took a course called Computer Science Discoveries, and we used the [code.org](http://code.org) platform to learn. We learned how to design mobile app prototypes (basically draw Figma-like templates) and present our proof of concept as well as develop websites with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Vividly, I remember making a website on my hypothetical trip to China. I showed it to my uncle and he suggested I used a html header called <marquee> where the text moved from right to left off the screen and it came back in the other direction. Kind of what you see in the digital signs on the metro busses. I implemented this and yes it was cool.

A gap

Late 8th grade, I didn’t pursue anything computer science related for a long time and that was when COVID happened. Schools shut down worldwide, and we had to be isolated in our homes for about a couple years and do things remotely. The summer before I started 7th grade, my uncle gifted me a gaming laptop, and I played video games on it, especially Watch Dogs 2 during quarantine. Watch Dogs 2 is an open-world video game where a hacker group stops and exploits a corporation from doing mass surveillance. I was intrigued by the hacking and weirdly enough the server farms and large scale networks.

To be honest, I don’t know why I didn’t code during quarantine and played video games instead, but it’s something I regret.

High school

When I went to high school, I was in an international baccalaureate school and not only did they offer IB courses, but some AP courses as well. In particular they offered AP computer science and IB computer science, so I decided to take both of them cuz I wanted to get back to learning computer science and maximize the opportunity.

This was a big mistake after I was told that I be doing the same work TWICE! Both courses were similar, but if I had to choose, I’d go with IB computer science.

I took both courses so I can provide perspective.

AP Computer Science Principles

I started coding and pursuing computer science in 11th grade again where I took AP Computer Science Principles. We did app development on [code.org](http://code.org) using a very old version of Javascript and designing our own UI with mouse and keyboard by moving shapes around, typing text onto the component, switching between colors, etc. (yes, this is better than implementing it with HTML and CSS).

I took their AP exam.

I got a 2 on the exam and in Layman terms this was absolute dogsh*t.

I felt discouraged and thought that CS wasn’t for me. But hey, I’m taking IB Computer Science HL, and spoilers: I redeemed myself on that course.

Projects I remember doing: Calculator app, Personality Quiz, Mental Therapy/Mindfulness app

IB Computer Science HL

I remember that if we had to take IB Computer Science HL, our teacher needed to sign it and yes I convinced her I wanted to take it even though she’s like, “are you sure?”. She signed it anyways and said “keep working hard” or something like that.

I took IB computer science HL in 12th grade, my last year in high school.

In a nutshell the advice I can give to you is to learn Object-oriented programming, basic programming principles (variables, functions, etc.), and data structures + algorithms first.

For my computer science IA (Internal Assessment), I did a project using HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and Gmail API where the user enters a (spam) email on the web app and submits it. This searches for all the emails in the inbox with that email and batch deletes all them in Gmail.

Back then I tried using ChatGPT at its earliest versions to help me program my project because my tech nerd friends were also using it, and for me it was efficient. That was the turning point that embraced me to use AI. ChatGPT helped me debug, ChatGPT helped me learn, and ChatGPT helped program for me.

I can tell you it wasn’t a great IA, and this demonstrated my lack of experience with frontend and backend dev.

Why I majored in computer science in college

There are other factors like watching movies and TV shows, but honestly these 4 factors influenced me to major in computer science and led me to where I am.

  1. My uncle

    He worked at multiple tech companies and interacted with technology a lot. Hanging out with him insipred me to consume and embrace technology more, which made me curious about how technology works.

  2. Watch Dogs 2

    This made me interested with how digital systems are structured and operated in a real-like world. I was fascinated with the hacking and the plot. More importantly, I was intrigued with seeing the large amounts of server farms and networks in the game.

  3. High school

    After taking computer science in high school, I realized there was so much to learn about in the field, and doing so in college was going to be helpful for me. I’ve been consumed by technology so much, I believe learning computer sciene was going to be worth it, and college was going to teach me a lot more in depth. With emerging AI chatbots like ChatGPT, I was even more interested in learning more about computer science especially with artifical intelligence.

  4. The desire to be different

    Despite being Filipino and surrounded by a culture of family and family-friends who are mostly nurses and decided they want to pursue the medical field, I wanted to be unique. And part of this inspiration came from my uncle who decided to take a different route instead of the medical field.

    I didn’t want to conform to the cultural/societal norms pretty much like my uncle.

Why I am doing this

There’s nothing wrong with looking back in the past and remembering where you came from, but it’s wrong to live the past in the present and possibly the future.

Often, I still try to find my purpose in life, and I often ask myself, why I decided to choose a certain path than another. I believe my past shaped me to where I am, but I still feel lost and senseless about where I currently am—why I am doing what I do in college.

It’s hard to forget about the past, and I’m still trying to overcome it. But when we ask ourselves what we’re meant to do, we need to look back to remember what we did and where it took us in life—the actions and motivations I took could’ve led to various paths and possibilities that I could’ve pursue.

I could’ve took a path that was independent from what I did in the past, but to me it feels purposeless. It’s like, why did i decided to pursue this path without thinking twice or having a past that motivated and drove me to that path?

That’s my take, but yeah.

TLDR: Currently, I feel like I am still trying to find my purpose, and looking back at my past is the way to explore what my younger-self wanted to do in the future as well as see how it might’ve led to where I am today.
