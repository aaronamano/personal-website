How watching Bluelock changed me

May 3, 2026

It turns out that watching anime wasn't for entertainment. It was meant to give life lessons.

If you don't know what Bluelock is, it's a soccer anime where, Isagi Yoichi, a high school soccer player, along with 299 other cracked high school soccer players are invited to a training camp program called Bluelock where the training camp manager, Jinpachi Ego, desires to make the best striker out of the 299 people including Isagi for the Japanese national soccer team.

Lessons I learned

  1. Learn how to adapt:

    Isagi's journey was a rough from being a low level ranked player to a star that beat the U-20 Japanese team. Each game, he was able to learn how to adapt based on the unexpected conditions and challenges he faced. If things didn't go his way, he was able to find a way through it. I loved the animation where his face was decomposed as a puzzle, and this showed how he would adapt to play with other players in the field.

  2. Embrace change:

    Similarly to number 1, embracing change means also adapting to new things. If a play didn't go his way, he would think of a new one on the spot. Personally, this would mean of trying to get rid of my old habits, doing something new, taking risks, and not be that person that I once was in the past. For Isagi, he had to embrace change by having a strong ego (as what Jinpachi taught them) and learning how to be tough in order to become the best striker in Japan. Of course, Isagi doesn't play the same way each game since each condition is different (playing the same way doesn't work all the time), but embracing change and adaptability go hand in hand.

  3. Failure makes you stronger:

    Of course, Isagi went through a lot, but a particular moment caught my eye. In the 3v3 match (Nagi, Isagi, and Bachira vs Rin, Tomikitsu, and Aryu), Isagi's team lost against the number 1 ranked player, Rin. And from the rules the winning team was able to choose a player from a losing team to join the winning team. Rin's team chose Bachira and Isagi was infuriated since him and Nagi had to rematch with another team to advance to the next stage, but were potentially outnumbered. This in my opinion was a significant failure, in Isagi's time at Bluelock, but this gave him the fuel to be stronger and have a goal to beat Rin. As for the rematch, Isagi and Nagi played against Barou and Naruhaya, and Isagi and Nagi won. They picked Barou, no surprise. This was a moment where Isagi felt worthless and disrespected (I cover this in number 6), however that didn't stop him. In my life, having lots of rejections and bombed interviews (even mock ones) discourage me from even applying and practing for interviews, but it is a great way for me to learn early from my mistakes.

  4. There must be bad in order for there to be good:

    This is a topic that could be controversial, but in Bluelock, players are eliminatd from each challenge, so only the best of the best are remaining at each progression. Isagi's character was shaped from being a soft, depressed, frustrated, laid-back soccer player to being a hard, egoistic, confident, and highly determined. Jinpachi Ego may be harsh, but that shaped people to who they are. I think influencing someone to have such a big ego isn't right, but it has shaped them to being a step close to a successful Japanese striker. Lots of sacrifices have to be made in order to achieve something. At least for me, skipping class in order to attend a workshop or networking event to help me grow is worthwhile. Potentially in hackathons, I might shift from working with beginners and newbies to more experienced hackers. Making a sacrifice like this for a probable gain is bittersweet. There could be exceptions where I still work with beginners though. Maybe there may be a time I would need to do something bad or make a huge sacrifice just to be able to stand out and become closer to being successful.

  5. Surround yourself in a great environment:

    Bachira's backstory inspired me to talk about this advice. He's a very talented dribbler, but he was lonely as a child because he was too good at soccer and was socially weird. As a result, he created an imaginary friend called his monster where he envisioned the monster playing with an opponent as strong as Bachira. The monster would influenced his decisions and playmaking in order to play better. With him coming to Bluelock, he was able to play with strong and cracked players as good as him, which led him from not listening to his monster as much as he did before. He strived to finding his monster in real life, and that's the environment Bachira genuinely enjoyed surrounding himself with. Turns out that Bachira still has that dawg (monster) in him, but it doesn't really control and overwhelm him like it did in his past. This is somethings I strive on doing—being surrounded by (cracked) people who love to build and learn about AI.

  6. Respect and status are earned:

    The important conflict is Isagi trying to be as strong as Rin. After Isagi's loss against him, he had one goal in mind: to defeat Rin and earn his respect and approval. As for me, I need to stand out by working hard, building my personal brand, networking, and showing that I'm a reliable asset to others. This in return would attract other people and perhaps they would reach out to me instead of the other way around. For Isagi, he needed to stand out so he can be noticed by Jinpachi Ego especially Rin by climbing the ranks. Isagi had to show that he was good by playing to his strengths, overcoming the tests Jinpachi indirectly placed, implementing them on the field, and even showing ego. By being better and proving you are reliable on the field, lots of players can respect you, and that high status comes with it.
