Navigating through noise

May 8, 2026

As an average CS major, I've seen a lot of noise lately, and in my humble opinion I believe that neglecting most of it will lead me to the right direction.

AI Slop

I became aware about differentating noise vs signal in general. However, I became very particular of this concept in the age of AI. You've heard the term AI slop. This could be a vibecoded project with multiple AI features that aren't coherent. I also heard the term of GPT wrappers and this is part of the AI slop.

What I learned to avoid making GPT wrappers

I became conscious of building projects using GPT wrappers. My description of a GPT wrapper is a UI built on top of an LLM API, which you call and it generates a response from the backend to the frontend.

  • Integrate 3rd party apps
  • Integrate real data from the internet and even leverage mock data from your database for your LLM
  • Asking yourself: is this something you could've done on the app itself (e.g. ChatGPT) instead of a building a UI for it?

How I try to keep up with AI

Many people want to keep up with AI because it is rapidly evolving. For me, this was keeping up with the latest technologies and frameworks with the word AI on it. People would do the same too to be fair lol. I almost became a victim of this trap.

  • Avoid jumping to the latest framework: I tend to choose LangChain/LangGraph. In the future I'd use Mastra, Google Vertex AI, and/or Google ADK. That is enough for me lol.
  • Learn fundamentals: This one is difficult since AI is evolving fast, but I'd focus on RAG, Vector Embeddings, etc. I am a firm believer that fundamentals never change but technologies and frameworks do. Building a solid foundation helps in the long run.
  • Reading research papers: This is similar to learning fundamentals, but the best way to learn about AI is reading research papers since they lay a foundation in AI like the ones from Stanford and other papers online. Company-based research papers like Anthropic, NVIDIA, and Google Deepmind are also fantastic.
  • On new AI frameworks and tech: If I see a new AI framework for example in a hackathon, I do research, see what stands out, and if it's worth using. I'd also try it out and see if it's convenient than what I usually use

Social Media

LinkedIn and Twitter have lowk been platforms where people literally post for engagement bait. In my opinion, I hate how social media has evolved in a way people passively make AI generated posts and captions in order try to break the algorithm to receive lots of engagement. Of course in my opinion, this is a lot of noise and BS. They say stuff like "comment x down below for y", "the first x people will receive y", etc. etc. It's something I noticed a lot, which is why I deleted LinkedIn. It personally doesn't help me.

How I try to differentiate between noise and signal on social media

  • If I see something that looks AI-generated or a caption that looks like it's for engagement bait, I avoid ts.
  • I follow big tech companies for their updates. I follow Google Cloud, NVIDIA, etc. where I could get legit opportunites, events, and content that they post.
  • Like I previously mentioned, I delete LinkedIn since it's become a fest of engagement bait posts
  • For advice, this varies. I posted Linkedin advice when I first started, but now it's useless. I usually listen to advice if it's personal/authentic/unique, insider information from companies people worked at, or perhaps interview advice if it affects me and could be valuable

Anyways, that pretty much it. Even though I'm still navigating what noise I should cut out and be open to what stuff would be considered signal to me and opportunistic, I try to learn new things and be open-minded to them.
